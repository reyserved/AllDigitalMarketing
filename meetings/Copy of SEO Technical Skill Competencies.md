**Classification Levels:**

* ***Unskilled (Junior SEO):*** Lacks foundational knowledge or misapplies concepts.  
* ***Skilled (SEO Specialist):*** Applies best practices accurately and aligns work with goals.  
* ***Master (SEO Manager):*** Leads strategy, teaches others, forecasts results.  
* ***Overused:*** Skill is misapplied or used excessively, providing diminishing or no ROI.

**Competency List:**

1. **Keyword Research**  
   *Definition: The ability to identify high quality keywords relevant to the business and aligning with user search intent.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Unable to differentiate between Purchase, Commercial, & Research keywords  
      2. Pulls keywords not relevant to the client’s industry or location  
      3. Does not verify intent or search landscape  
      4. Relies on a single tool for executing keyword research   
      5. Does not format and or organize deliverables for internal use  
      6. Overindexes on keyword volume without taking relevance, intent, or competition into consideration  
      7. Fails to check for SERP features (maps, featured snippets).  
      8. Confuses branded vs. non-branded keywords  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can differentiate between Purchase, Commercial, & Research keywords  
      2. Pulls keywords relevant to the client’s industry or location  
      3. Verifies keyword intent in search landscape outside research tool  
      4. Uses multiple tools when executing keyword research  
      5. Formats and organizes deliverables effectively for internal use  
      6. Can map keywords to current site structure  
      7. Can categorize keywords into Brand, Variant, Core, Supporting, \+Modifier  
      8. Can identify semantic keywords   
   3. Master:  
      1. Can identify page builds or content clusters missing from the current site structure  
      2. Can identify search trends, seasonality, and algorithm  
      3. Can forecast business impact from keyword strategies  
      4. Can optimize for semantically related keywords with nuanced intent differences, using SERP analysis to guide keyword targeting and content strategy  
      5. Can identify paid search opportunities  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Over builds keyword research doc with every potential keyword found in the research  
      2. Compiling excessively large "kitchen sink" keyword lists with thousands of terms that are never used.  
      3. Obsessively pursuing high-volume, high-difficulty keywords where the brand has no realistic chance of ranking in the near term.  
      4. Conducting frequent, minor revisions to keyword lists without a clear strategic trigger (product launch, market shift).  
      5. Focusing on minute fluctuations in keyword search volume instead of broader trends.  
2. **Technical SEO**  
   *Definition: The ability to build and maintain a site infrastructure that supports crawling, indexing, and ranking, optimized through improvements to structure, speed, mobile usability, and secure protocols.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Cannot explain fundamental technical concepts like crawling vs. indexing or the role of a sitemap.  
      2. Neglects to identify or incorrectly implements critical indexing controls (noindex tags, canonicals, robots.txt directives).  
      3. Does not understand the concept of crawl budget or how to optimize it  
      4. Ignores mobile-first indexing principles and fails to test for mobile usability issues.  
      5. Fails to identify and diagnose common site errors like 404s, 501s, redirect chains, or soft 404s.  
      6. Poorly communicates technical requirements to developers using vague language without clear, actionable direction.  
      7. Misunderstands how site architecture, URL structure, and internal linking impact crawlability.  
      8. Struggles to diagnose the root causes of poor site speed and Core Web Vitals  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Conducts technical SEO audits using multiple tools (Screaming Frog, SEM Rush, Google Search Console) and manually verifies presence of issues.   
      2. Can develop/implement clear strategies to improve CWV and overall page speed.  
      3. Can develop/implement effective relevant structured data (LocalBusiness, Organization, Service) to enhance SERP visibility.  
      4. Can manage a site's indexing strategy through proper use of canonicalization, robots.txt, and XML sitemaps.  
      5. Can develop/implement for a logical, scalable site architecture and clean URL structures.  
      6. Can manage  HTTPS implementation and follows site security best practices  
      7. Can write clear and actionable instructions for development teams.  
      8. Can execute full mobile usability and compliance with mobile-first indexing standards.  
      9. Can locate the robots.txt and XML sitemap files, detecting associated errors, and identifying the application or platform that generated them  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can translate technical SEO issues into business risks and opportunities ("Improving LCP by 0.5s is projected to increase conversion rates by 3%").  
      2. Can lead technical SEO strategy for complex projects like site migrations, location expansion, or platform changes, anticipating and mitigating risks.  
      3. Can coach development teams on SEO best practices, integrating technical SEO into the sprint planning and QA process.  
      4. Can develop advanced strategies for script-heavy websites.  
      5. Can integrate technical SEO insights into broader digital strategies, advising on technology stack choices and platform capabilities.  
      6. Can integrate technical SEO insights into broader content or on-page strategies.   
   4. Overused:  
      1. Obsessively focusing on minor, low-impact technical factors that have little-to-no measurable effect on rankings (debating \<strong\> vs \<b\> tags).  
      2. Producing exhaustive, multi-hundred-page technical audits without clear prioritization, leading to analysis paralysis for the development team.  
      3. Constantly tweaking minor technical elements without a defined strategic purpose.  
      4. Chasing a perfect score (100/100) on performance tools at the expense of actual user experience or business goals.  
3. **On-Page SEO**  
   *Definition: The ability to optimize individual pages (content, metadata, headings, URL structure) for keyword relevance, user experience, and search visibility.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Creates content with a flat structure, misusing or ignoring heading tags (H1-H6).  
      2. Writes generic, untargeted metadata (title tags, meta descriptions) that fails to attract clicks.  
      3. Practices "keyword stuffing," forcing keywords into content unnaturally.  
      4. Relies on outdated concepts like "keyword density" as a primary optimization metric.  
      5. Uploads large, uncompressed images without descriptive file names or alt text.  
      6. Does not add internal links or adds them in an illogical, unhelpful way.  
      7. Produces content with poor readability (long blocks of text, no formatting) that creates a poor user experience (UX).  
      8. Fails to align the page's content and format with the dominant search intent for the target query.  
      9. Fails to understand the difference between “evergreen” content and pages that require updates or content refreshes (based on context, intent, and changes to legal system).  
      10. Doesn’t review or edit AI produced content.   
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can write compelling title tags and meta descriptions, optimized for both search engines and user CTR.  
      2. Uses a logical heading structure (H1s, H2s, H3s) to improve readability and Google's comprehension.  
      3. Can strategically implement internal links to distribute page authority and guide users to relevant content.  
      4. Can optimize images for file size, descriptive alt text, and relevance.  
      5. Balances natural keyword integration with a strong focus on readability and user experience.  
      6. Can naturally integrate keyword targets into key page elements (title tag, H1, first paragraph) following best practices.   
      7. Understands and implements signals of E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust) such as citing sources, social proof, clear contact info and location addresses.  
      8. Can optimize pages for specific SERP features like Featured Snippets by using clear questions and answers, lists, and tables.  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can create comprehensive on-page SEO guidelines and training materials for content generation.  
      2. Can integrate on-page optimizations into a holistic content strategy that builds topical authority.  
      3. Uses A/B testing and analytics to refine on-page elements (like titles, headers, and calls-to-action) for maximum CTR and conversion.  
      4. Proactively adapts on-page strategies in response to algorithm updates and shifts in how search engines interpret content.  
      5. Can coach teams on advanced concepts like semantic SEO, entity optimization (NER), and natural language processing (NLP) to create content that aligns with user intent.  
      6. Can implement and use heatmaps, session recordings, and GA4 to diagnose and improve how users interact with page layouts and content.  
      7. Can leverage outside tools and resources to automate on-page optimization at scale.  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Excessive tweaking of minor on-page elements (e.g., re-ordering words in a title tag weekly) with no goal aligned with our strategy.  
      2. Overly focusing on keyword frequency or density, compromising content quality and readability, resulting in diminishing returns.  
      3. Rewriting an entire page for a minor keyword variation instead of consolidating or expanding the existing content.  
      4. Manually optimizing alt text for purely decorative images that provide no contextual value.  
4. **Content Strategy & Optimization**  
   *Definition: The ability to plan, create, and refine content to match user intent, meet SEO goals, and enhance user engagement.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Creates content on an ad-hoc basis without a documented strategy or clear business objectives.  
      2. Works without a defined target audience, resulting in generic or irrelevant content.  
      3. Does not align copy and format with the primary search intent of the target query.  
      4. Produces poorly structured content that is difficult to read and navigate.  
      5. Fails to plan a content calendar or distinguish between evergreen vs. seasonal pages.  
      6. Doesn't use a process for publishing new or auditing existing content for updates.  
      7. Doesn’t have a foundational understanding of the business and practice areas.  
      8. Fails to use data or search trends during strategy or content creation.  
      9. Does not measure or report on content performance beyond basic page views.  
      10. Struggles to prioritize content efforts, often focusing on low-value topics.  
      11. Ineffective at communicating the value or purpose of the content strategy to stakeholders.  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can develop a documented content strategy that is directly tied to business goals and marketing KPIs.  
      2. Can build detailed audience personas and maps content to their journey and search intent.  
      3. Can create data-driven content calendars based on keyword research, topic clusters, and competitor analysis.  
      4. Can conduct regular content audits to identify gaps, opportunities for updates, and underperforming assets.  
      5. Can use analytics and user behavior metrics (e.g., bounce rate, scroll depth, heatmaps) to inform content improvements.  
      6. Can strategically repurpose and reformat content for different channels and SERP features (e.g., turning a blog post into a video script or infographic).  
      7. Can collaborate with subject matter experts (SMEs), designers, and other stakeholders to produce high quality content.  
      8. Can track and report on content performance, connecting metrics to ROI and business impact.  
      9. Can ensure content matches intent and answers user questions, demonstrating strong E-E-A-T signals.  
      10. Can actively manage a "content hub" (topic cluster) model to build authority.  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can develop predictive models to forecast the impact of a content strategy on traffic and conversions.  
      2. Builds a "programmatic content" or "content at scale" engine for certain niches, using templates and data to create high quality pages efficiently.  
      3. Can coach and lead entire marketing teams on semantic SEO and topical authority.  
      4. Can build scalable systems for content creation, audits, and measurement across a large organization.  
      5. Can align the SEO content strategy with the overarching brand voice, PR, and authority building initiatives to maximize E-E-A-T.  
      6. Pioneers new content formats (format, style, tool) to gain a competitive edge.  
      7. Can strategize and build a content cluster beyond 3 levels without diluting topical authority.    
   4. Overused:  
      1. Creating low quality blog posts just to hit a publication cadence.  
      2. Over focusing on word count as a primary measure of quality, leading to bloated and unfocused articles.  
      3. Creating overly complex content strategy documents that are never implemented.  
      4. Making constant, minor copy edits to pages based on small keyword ranking fluctuations rather than user engagement data.  
5. **Off-Page SEO & Link Building**  
   *Definition: The ability to build authority and trust by acquiring high quality backlinks, mentions, and external signals (reviews, social media, local citations, NAP consistency).*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Acquires backlinks from spammy websites.  
      2. Cannot differentiate between a valuable link and a potentially harmful one.  
      3. Uses outdated and risky tactics such as GSA or exact-match anchor text stuffing.  
      4. Does not vary anchor text, leading to an unnatural backlink profile.  
      5. Fails to monitor the backlink profile for new, lost, or toxic links.  
      6. Misjudges a website's authority based solely on a single third-party metric (like DA/DR) without manual review.  
      7. Is unaware of or completely disregards Google's guidelines on link building/schemes.  
      8. Cannot explain the strategic goals of a link-building campaign.  
      9. Fails to align link-building efforts with content marketing or PR campaigns.  
      10. Uses the GSC disavow tool.  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can earn backlinks from relevant, authoritative websites through targeted outreach and relationship building.  
      2. Can perform competitor backlink analysis to identify strategic opportunities and replicable tactics.  
      3. Can conduct regular backlink audits and identify content or link opportunities.   
      4. Can use a natural mix of branded, naked, and partial-match anchor text.  
      5. Can collaborate with the content team to create "link-worthy" assets (e.g., data studies, infographics, calculators, other tools).  
      6. Can clearly communicate the strategy, risks, and expected outcomes of link-building campaigns.  
      7. Can integrate off-page SEO with brand-building and PR strategies.  
      8. Understands the pros and cons of link building opportunities (e.g., strategic guest posting, broken link building, unlinked brand mention reclamation.)  
      9. Can leverage digital PR and social signals to amplify content and attract links.  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can design and execute large-scale, creative link-building campaigns that generate top-tier media coverage and backlinks.  
      2. Can develop a predictive model for the ROI of a link-building campaign, tying link acquisition costs to projected traffic value.  
      3. Can coach and build teams focused on scalable outreach, digital PR, and relationship management.  
      4. Can build automated systems for prospecting, monitoring, and reporting on link-building efforts.  
      5. Can proactively adapt link-building strategies based on industry changes and competitor activities.  
      6. Can connect link-building directly to brand reputation management and E-E-A-T enhancement.  
      7. Can build relationships with journalists, bloggers, and industry influencers to generate natural mentions and links.  
      8. Analyzes the ROI of link building tactics and adapts strategies to emerging trends, such as the growing importance of unlinked brand mentions in AI-powered search  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Prioritizing the sheer quantity of links over their quality and relevance.  
      2. Obsessively chasing a higher Domain Authority (DA) or Domain Rating (DR) score without considering if the links are driving relevant traffic or topical authority.  
      3. Spending excessive time on low-ROI tactics like GSA, non-citation directory submissions, or forum commenting.  
      4. Over-monitoring daily backlink fluctuations and takes action on minor spam links that Google likely already ignores.  
6. **Analytics & Reporting**  
   *Definition: The ability to measure and interpret SEO performance data to uncover insights, guide strategy, and connect SEO efforts to measurable business outcomes.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Misunderstands the meaning of basic metrics like users, sessions, bounce rate, and events.  
      2. Produces "data-puke" reports with tables and charts but no insights, context, or recommendations.  
      3. Fails to implement tracking consistently or correctly, leading to untrustworthy data.  
      4. Fails to integrate data from other sources like Google Search Console, relying only on a single platform.  
      5. Cannot explain the "why" behind data trends or performance changes.  
      6. Does not use segmentation, analyzing all data in aggregate.  
      7. Confuses correlation with causation when interpreting data.  
      8. Fails to extract actionable insights from data; can only state what happened, not what to do next.  
      9. Cannot connect SEO performance metrics to tangible business impact (e.g., leads, sales).  
      10. Fails to consider historical data and seasonality when analyzing performance.  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can accurately track and report on key SEO KPIs, including organic traffic, rankings, conversions, and user engagement.  
      2. Can build clear, concise, and actionable reports tailored to the audience (e.g., executive summary vs. detailed tactical report).  
      3. Can cross-validate data across multiple platforms (e.g., GA4, GSC, Ahrefs) to ensure accuracy.  
      4. Can clearly explain performance trends, providing context and data-backed hypotheses for changes.  
      5. Can effectively segment data by channel, device, location, landing page, and audience type to uncover deeper insights.  
      6. Can set up and track goals, events, and conversions accurately within analytics platforms.  
      7. Can connect SEO performance directly to business outcomes and calculate the ROI of SEO initiatives.  
      8. Can regularly audit data integrity and tracking implementation.  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can develop sophisticated forecasting models to predict future SEO performance and set realistic targets.  
      2. Can build advanced, automated dashboards (e.g., in Looker Studio, Tableau) that integrate SEO data with other business intelligence (BI) systems.  
      3. Can coach and mentor others on how to interpret data, find insights, and use analytics for strategic decision-making.  
      4. Can lead the integration of SEO data into the central marketing technology stack (e.g., marketing platforms, workflows).  
      5. Can use data storytelling to communicate the strategic value and impact of SEO to C-level executives.  
      6. Can proactively identify strategic adjustments based on predictive signals and micro-trends in the data.  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Reporting on a long list of irrelevant or "vanity" metrics that don't inform strategy.  
      2. Spending more time formatting reports than analyzing the data within them.  
      3. Overcomplicating reports with excessive charts and data points that obscure the key message.  
      4. Constantly revising report formats and KPIs without a strategic reason, making it impossible to track long-term trends.  
7. **Local SEO & Google Maps**  
   *Definition: The ability to optimize a business's Share of Local Voice (SoLV) in Google Maps and locally relevant results through GBP management, citations, reviews, and geo-specific content.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Does not understand the three pillars of local ranking: proximity, prominence, and relevance.  
      2. Lacks a clear understanding Share of Local Voice (SoLV) or how to properly configure/utilize it  
      3. Fails to set up a Google Business Profile (GBP) with complete or accurate information, e.g., select appropriate GBP categories, or utilize features like services and descriptions, posts, Q\&A, and detailed descriptions.  
      4. Fails to maintain consistent Name, Address, and Phone number (NAP) information across the web.  
      5. Fails to manage online reviews or responds to them unprofessionally.  
      6. Cannot identify and resolve duplicate GBP listings.  
      7. Does not track local rankings or competitor presence in the Map Pack.  
      8. Fails to create locally-focused content or service pages.  
      9. Does not build local citations or pursue local link-building opportunities.  
      10. Cannot track performance or connect local SEO efforts to business results (e.g., phone calls, store visits).  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can fully optimize and maintain a Google Business Profile, utilizing all relevant features.  
      2. Can conduct regular audits to ensure NAP and citation consistency across key directories.  
      3. Can target local keywords strategically on the website, including in titles, headers, and on city/service-specific landing pages.  
      4. Can implement an effective strategy to solicit and respond to online reviews.  
      5. Can analyze local competitors' strategies to identify opportunities.  
      6. Can build relevant local citations and acquire backlinks from local businesses or organizations.  
      7. Can track and report on Map Pack rankings, local organic traffic, and GBP engagement metrics.  
      8. Can implement local business schema markup on the website.  
      9. Demonstrates a strong understanding of SoLV and AGR, including their implementation.  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can design and execute multi-location or enterprise-level local SEO strategies for franchise businesses.  
      2. Can proactively adapt local strategies based on algorithm updates and changes to the GBP platform.  
      3. Can train clients on local SEO best practices and reputation management.  
      4. Can forecast the potential business impact (e.g., increase in calls, bookings) from improvements in local visibility.  
      5. Can integrate local SEO with other marketing efforts, such as local paid search, social media campaigns, and in-store or offline promotions.  
      6. Can innovate with advanced local tactics like geo-fencing, proximity marketing, and sophisticated schema for multi-department businesses.  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Obsessively checking local rankings daily, which are highly personalized and prone to fluctuation.  
      2. Prioritizing the sheer quantity of citations from low-quality directories over the quality and accuracy of core listings.  
      3. Overcomplicating local tactics for a single-location business that only needs a solid foundation.  
      4. Spending too much time tracking minor competitors in a different service area or with a different business model.  
8. **SEO Strategy & Project Management**  
   *Definition: The ability to align SEO initiatives with business goals & waterfall metrics through planning, prioritization, cross-functional execution, and ongoing project coordination.*  
   1. Unskilled:  
      1. Fails to develop a documented SEO strategy or defined objectives.  
      2. Fails to manage timelines, resources, and budgets effectively.  
      3. Cannot effectively prioritize tasks, often focusing on low-impact activities.  
      4. Fails to track or document work, progress, and results.  
      5. Fails to communicate project dependencies, risks, and status updates clearly.  
      6. Does not align SEO goals with broader marketing campaigns.  
      7. Fails to manage stakeholder expectations or communicate effectively.  
      8. Does not use a systematic approach to planning and execution.  
      9. Fails to monitor or report on performance to validate the strategy.  
      10. Fails to collaborate effectively with cross-functional teams (dev, content, UX, paid).  
   2. Skilled:  
      1. Can define and document clear, measurable SEO goals and KPIs that align with business objectives.  
      2. Can build realistic and effective project plans, roadmaps, and timelines.  
      3. Can prioritize initiatives by evaluating impact (value) vs. effort (time, cost).  
      4. Can communicate progress, roadblocks, and results to stakeholders in a clear and timely manner.  
      5. Can track all work and results using project management tools (e.g., ClickUp).  
      6. Can coordinate effectively with developers, content creators, UX designers, and other teams to ensure successful execution.  
      7. Can manage client or stakeholder expectations effectively.  
      8. Recognizes the importance of Waterfall metrics and their role in analytics, helping to inform efforts that support business growth  
   3. Master:  
      1. Can develop and champion a long-term SEO vision that aligns with the company's multi-year business and marketing strategy.  
      2. Can build business cases for SEO initiatives, forecasting potential impact, resource requirements, and risks.  
      3. Can coach and mentor teams on agile SEO workflows and effective project management.  
      4. Can act as an advocate for SEO at the executive level, securing buy-in and resources for strategic initiatives.  
      5. Can integrate SEO planning into the core product development and marketing campaign lifecycle.  
      6. Can develop scalable processes and frameworks that enable a growing team to execute effectively.  
      7. Strategically aligns and integrates Waterfall metrics with advanced analytics to drive business growth  
      8. Can hold ultimate accountability for the SEO program's contribution to top-line revenue and business growth.  
   4. Overused:  
      1. Spending more time on documentation and planning ("analysis paralysis") than on execution.  
      2. Creating overly rigid and complex strategies that cannot adapt to changing priorities or market conditions.  
      3. Operating the SEO program in a strategic vacuum, disconnected from the primary goals of the business.  
      4. Constantly shifting priorities without allowing initiatives enough time to mature and show results.
