For paid traffic, a grid alone can feel like a menu. Use it as a selector, then give each area a **mini story**:

* **What you’re dealing with** (1–2 lines of empathy \+ stakes)

* **CTA** (“Talk to a lawyer about your \[topic\]”)

This is how you keep breadth *and* maintain clarity.

## **Paid ads landing page best practices: what’s working vs. what to tighten**

### **What aligns well**

* **Above-the-fold CTA** (“Get a Free Consultation”) and clear positioning (Georgia, family law).

* **Trust bar** with scannable value props (experience, local expertise, “we fight”).

* **Process section \+ FAQ** reduces anxiety and objections (great for family law where fear/uncertainty is high).

* **Emergency mention** can capture urgent cases (but see compliance/UX note below).

### **What’s not quite “paid ads best practice” yet (high impact fixes)**

1. **Message match / intent match is too broad**  
    If the ad/keyword is “divorce lawyer Atlanta” but the page shows 10 practice areas, users hesitate. Best practice is typically **one intent per page** (Divorce, Custody, Support Modification, etc.), with only light cross-links to others.

2. **Not enough “proof” (trust signals) for a high-stakes purchase**  
    You have claims (“proven track record,” “we fight”), but paid traffic converts better when you add:

   * Review score \+ count (Google/Avvo/etc.)

   * Awards/memberships (if real)

   * Short testimonials (especially about responsiveness \+ courtroom readiness)

   * Case outcomes *carefully phrased* (“results vary”)  
      Right now it’s mostly assertion, not evidence.

3. **CTA consistency \+ friction reduction**  
    You use both **“Free Consultation”** and **“Free Case Evaluation.”** Pick one term and repeat it everywhere (button labels, sticky CTA, closing section).  
    Also: add “What happens next?” microcopy near the form (e.g., “We’ll call within X business hours”) and “Confidential • No obligation.”

4. **Add a second primary conversion path: click-to-call**  
    In family law, many high-intent visitors prefer calling (especially on mobile). Even if the *primary* goal is form fill, include a **prominent call option** (sticky mobile bar).

5. **Choice overload in practice-area grid**  
    A grid is fine for SEO or a general services page, but on paid landers it can become a “menu” that leaks conversions. Consider: show **only the top 3–5** most relevant to the campaign, with a “See all family law services” accordion.

## **StoryBrand (SB7) alignment: you’re close, but the “story” needs clearer stakes \+ a sharper plan**

StoryBrand’s core is: **character → problem → guide → plan → CTA → avoid failure → success.**

Here’s how your current draft maps:

* **Character (customer as hero):** implied (“Your Family, Your Future, Your Rights”), but could be more *specific* (“You want time with your kids,” “you want a fair settlement”).

* **Problem:** you mention emotional/legal complexity; good start.

* **Guide:** present (experience \+ compassion \+ strength).

* **Plan:** you have a 6-step process; that’s strong, but SB7 usually benefits from a **simple 3-step plan** (less cognitive load):

  1. Book a consult

  2. Get a strategy

  3. Take action (negotiate or litigate)

* **CTA:** clear.

* **Avoid failure:** *lightly* present (“Don’t leave your future to chance”), but you should spell out stakes in a calm, ethical way (no fear-mongering): “costly paperwork mistakes,” “unfair custody schedule,” “financial terms that follow you for years.”

* **Success:** you hint at “move forward,” but add a vivid “after” picture: “a parenting plan you can live with,” “clear court orders,” “peace of mind,” “a path forward.”

### **StoryBrand tweak that also fits the “fighter” brand**

Position the attorney as a **calm, strategic fighter**:

* “We negotiate from strength—prepared to go to court if that’s what it takes.”

* “Firm when needed, measured always.”

That matches your “compassion and strength” line, but makes the “fighter” trait feel *competent*, not combative.

## **Compliance/brand safety quick notes (Georgia \+ platforms)**

Not legal advice, but two practical cautions:

* Georgia (like many states) emphasizes ads must not be misleading; consider adding **prominent disclaimers** (e.g., “Prior results do not guarantee similar outcomes,” “Attorney-client relationship only after signed agreement”).

* If you say “Free Consultation,” make sure it’s accurate and properly disclosed/limited if required by your jurisdiction’s guidance.  
   Also consider adding: “If you’re in immediate danger, call 911” near the emergency filing language for safety/clarity.
