Why Planning Beats Predicting

During volatility, investors can feel compelled to act quickly. The risk is turning temporary stress into a permanent decision.

Use this guide to keep decisions aligned with your plan—not to forecast markets.

Markets and economies are uncertain. Declines can deepen or last longer than expected. Losses are possible, and no strategy guarantees a positive outcome.

The Bear Market Discipline Framework
(4 Steps)

Use this sequence to keep decisions grounded in your plan.

Step 1: Preserve Liquidity

Map the next 12–24 months of cash needs and identified funding sources to reduce the likelihood of selling long-term assets at depressed prices.

Step 2: Reconfirm Risk

Review concentration, portfolio drivers, illiquid holdings, and time-horizon alignment to confirm risk still matches goals and capacity.

Step 3: Coordinate Taxes and Cash Flow

Review gains, losses, distribution needs, charitable plans, and upcoming tax payments with your advisor and CPA before making changes.

Step 4: Install Behavioral Guardrails

Use a written process, a cooling-off period, and clear rationale requirements to reduce headline-driven decisions.

Before, During, and After Volatility

The guide includes practical checklists for each phase.

Before Volatility

  • Confirm a 12–24 month liquidity plan.
  • Review concentration and diversification.
  • Document risk targets and your rebalancing approach.
  • Set a cadence for structured reviews.

During Volatility

  • Revisit liquidity first.
  • Avoid major shifts without a cooling-off period and written rationale.
  • Coordinate tax-related actions with the appropriate professionals.
  • Reduce noise and focus on what you can control.

After Volatility

  • Reassess risk alignment and portfolio drift.
  • Update your written plan with lessons learned.
  • Rebuild reserves if used.
  • Schedule a structured, non-emotional review.

Decision Filter and One-Page Action Plan

Apply the Decision Filter before changing strategy.

Decision Filter

  1. Did your life change? (for example: income, health, business event, major purchase, new dependents, legal change)
  2. Did your goals or time horizon change?
  3. Are you reacting primarily to fear or headlines?
  4. If markets declined another 10%–15%, would you still be comfortable with the new strategy?

Changes are most defensible when they’re tied to your plan and circumstances—not the news cycle.

  • Next 12–24 months cash needs.
  • Identified liquidity sources.
  • Most significant risk exposures.

Prepare for Your Advisor and CPA Conversation

Bring clear questions and the right documents so decisions are coordinated.

Questions for Your Advisor

  • How well does my 12–24 month liquidity plan hold up under different scenarios?
  • Where is my concentration risk, and what are options to reduce it over time?
  • What is our rebalancing policy (bands and frequency) during periods of stress?
  • Which tax-aware moves are worth exploring now, subject to CPA guidance?
  • Which behavioral guardrails should we formalize together?

Questions for Your CPA or Tax Professional

  • What is my year-to-date realized gain/loss picture?
  • Would tax-loss harvesting be beneficial in my situation, considering wash-sale rules?
  • How should charitable gifts be coordinated this year?
  • Do upcoming tax payments affect portfolio cash needs?

Documents to Have Ready

  • Most recent account statements.
  • Cash-flow summary for 12–24 months.
  • Tax documents relevant to realized gains/losses.
  • Charitable giving plans.
  • Any life changes since last review.

The Role of an Advisor During Volatility

Advisors don’t replace CPAs. They complement them.

  • Coordinate investment and tax decisions so potential moves are evaluated after tax.
  • Translate market outcomes into planning adjustments tied to goals and liquidity.
  • Model tradeoffs across risk, return, tax impact, and cash flow.
  • Help keep portfolio policy aligned with long-term objectives.
